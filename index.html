About Me

I am concurrently a Teaching Associate and a Research Fellow in the University of Nottingham branch of the Trustworthy Autonomous Systems research hub, previously working in the ENLIVEN European project. As much as I dream that it would mean a 50%/50% workload split, it typically means that I end up with a 200% workload. This page serves as a point of contact to find me.

I did my PhD in the Robert Gordon University, with a SICSA Prize studentship to research argumentation stance mining in social media. I have been lucky to hold many different roles, from software engineering and data science positions in a tech startup in Edinburgh to several research and teaching contracts in the University of Toulouse, the Robert Gordon University, and the University of Nottingham. I have worked in many teams and with a multitude of people of different backgrounds: software engineers, computer scientists, petroleum geologists, education researchers, and many I am forgetting, which has given me a taste for interdisciplinary research. If you share this taste and are looking for a collaboration, feel free to get in touch with me.

Research Interest

My scientific interests revolve around applied interdisciplinary research involving machine learning at its core. I am particularly interested and open to collaborative work in the following topics: interpretable machine learning, digital humanities (argument mining, social media mining), and digital health (physiological data, mental health). I am currently involved in the Coronavirus Discourses project, as well as other TAS Hub projects on public sentiment towards autonomous systems and moderation of public discourse.

About letters of recommendation

I have a bad case of the referral fatigue so I will look for any excuse to avoid writing them. You can easily trick me into writing them for you by doing the following:

  • Politely ask for them. If I receive a request from a university about an application I never heard about before, I will ignore it and you will look bad.
  • Don’t ask for too many of them. I take pride in my work, whether it is teaching or research, so letters of recommendation take time. Show me that you respect my time by asking for a couple of references instead of bombarding my name on a dozen of them.
  • Make sure that I know you. Me teaching a module is not enough, even if you got a good grade (a good grade is not indicative that you will be fit for a Masters or a PhD). Sending me your previous transcripts from high school is also not very convincing.
  • Show me that you know me. If you are asking me to write a reference letter for an engineering school, I will know that you have not even spent a minute researching what I do and what I am qualified to comment on. If you can’t do that little amount of research, how can I recommend you for something as significant as a postgraduate degree?
