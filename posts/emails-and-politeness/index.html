Sending e-mails and basic academic politeness

Jeff Leek has an excellent blog post from 2011 on getting e-mails responses from busy people. While his recommendations are quite strict, they are a good idea of general guidelines university students should pick up when trying to communicate with most academics, who are almost always busy people. His rules are the following:

  1. Limit the number of e-mails you send to that person to one a day.
  2. Limit the size of the e-mails to at most 3 sentences. Preferably shorter. I think this is extreme, but if you start thinking about paragraphs your e-mail is simply too long.
  3. Limit the format of questions to a yes/no answer. More generally, I think it's more about limiting the set of responses. It could be yes/no or it could be something else. You have to weigh in what kind of feedback you really need - it takes me about a minute to read a piece of text and answer good/needs improvement/needs major rewriting, but it takes me multiple hours to produce a nuanced response. I do not have multiple hours, so you need to decide whether you want a simple response today or a nuanced one next week.
  4. In case of time sensitive issues, state a default action and a date/time at which you will take that action unless advised otherwise. For example, "I will fix the grammar and send the report as is on Monday at 8 AM unless there is an issue".
  5. Be as specific as possible in your requests without going over in length. More specifically, don't email someone to ask them if you can e-mail them to ask a question. Just e-mail the question.
  6. Make sure your e-mail contains keywords that make it easy to search for them later. Most e-mail software is terrible - make sure your e-mail does not get lost by making its life easier.

Basic politeness

Now that this is said, the second thing is basic politeness. It’s a cultural feature so different rules might apply in different places, but while there are many correct ways of addressing academic staff as a student (some people prefer first name, some people prefer Dr. Lastname, some people prefer Professor, etc.) there are definitely incorrect ways of doing it.

  • “Dr. Firstname” makes it sound like you are addressing a TV personality.
  • Nicknames, unless the lecturer has told you that’s what they go by.
  • “Dude”/“bro” (actually seen in e-mails) is probably not a good idea.
