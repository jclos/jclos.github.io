What to expect

Because 2020 is the way it is, I am progressively shifting my focus to supervising projects for social good, i.e. projects which have a net positive or at least a net neutral ethical footprint. What that means in practice for you as a potential student, is that I will not supervise the following types of projects:

  • - Stock price prediction projects, because not only are they ethically dubious, they are boring;
  • - Anything related to optimising financial objectives ;
  • - Modelling COVID-19 - leave that to the epidemiologists.

On the other hand, I am particularly interested in projects which bring a net positive to the world, such as helping stop the spread of fake news, propaganda and hate speech online, or software to help mental health professionals analyse their therapy data, etc.

The following project ideas are separated in multiple categories. Engineering projects focus on producing software, and they will be judged according to the following rules of thumb: would someone hire me if I showed them that software? Would someone purchase this software? Research projects focus on designing, developing and running an experiment, and then extracting empirical knowledge from it. They are judged based on the following rules of thumb: could this be published in a conference or a scientific journal, and how much would be needed to make it so?

What do you need to do?

I put a lot of time and effort in supervising student projects and I expect you to match that effort, therefore the first thing you should do is ask yourself whether you really want to do that specific project or whether you are just choosing it because you are out of ideas. If you are still motivated by the idea, then you should contact me and tell me which project interests you and why, and your ideas of what it could entail, and why you think you can do a good job at it. I do not expect you to spit out a half finished project and have an appropriate CV, but a lot of those projects can only be done justice if you have some prior exposure to their topics, whether it is something you did on your own (or via some MOOC), or something you did in your university.

If you are looking into potentially pursuing a research postgraduate degree (MRes, PhD) I would highly recommend discussing it first with me. Some of those projects could easily end up in research papers and having research papers to your name is very good for your application to a graduate school.

Engineering projects

A Semi-Automated Fact-Checking Tool for Online News

Keywords: Fake News Detection Online News Web Programming

In this project you will build a browser plug-in which lets users label and annotate news articles with evidence of their veracity, or lack thereof. The system will also let users register accounts and assess other users as trustworthy or untrustworthy. This labelling will allow the system to propagate trustworthiness metrics along the graph of users, based on some existing graph algorithms in abstract argumentation theory.

An Explanation-Based Recipe Recommender System

Keywords: Recommender Systems Information Retrieval Web Programming

[Taken by a student. If you are interested get in touch and we can work on designing a variation of it]

In this project you will build a full-fledged recommender system based on a large scale collection of recipes. The aim of the recommender system is to recommend a healthier choice of food to the users. For instance, when a user search for a high calorie food recipe (e.g. spaghetti meatballs), the system should be able to recommend a healthier option to the user (e.g. skinny spaghetti meatballs) and provide an explanation on why this was recommended. In addition to healthier choice, the system should also take into account the user's profile (e.g. the amount of calories intake per day, health conditions like diabetes, hypertension, and food preference) when generating recommendations.

Users are able to search recipes by ingredients, speed, difficulty, type of cuisine, type of meals. On top of that, users are also able to filter based on criteria that matter to them such as vegan, gluten-free, high protein, etc. When returning recommendations, the system will provide the recommended recipe ingredients, steps to cook, image of the food and explanations for the recommendations given using different explanation approaches (e.g. nutrition facts, calorie counts, popularity, user preference) for each of the results (e.g., "result 1 was returned because it has a lower sugar content"). Users are able to 'like' or 'dislike' a recipe and provide an explanation of what guided their choice (e.g., "the picture looked appealing"). The recommender system will take that feedback into account to improve its recommendations.

The design of the explanation interface aims to achieve the following quality dimensions: Efficiency, Effectiveness, Transparency, Scrutability, Trust, Persuasiveness, Satisfaction (Tintarev, N. and Masthoff, J., 2007). Further, we are interested to investigate whether personalised explanation is necessary in the context of food recommender systems that promotes healthy eating.

Recommended reading:

  1. Tintarev, N. and Masthoff, J., 2007, April. A survey of explanations in recommender systems. In 2007 IEEE 23rd international conference on data engineering workshop (pp. 801-810). IEEE.
  2. Gedikli, F., Jannach, D. and Ge, M., 2014. How should I explain? A comparison of different explanation types for recommender systems. International Journal of Human-Computer Studies, 72(4), pp.367-382.
  3. Trattner, C. and Elsweiler, D., 2017. Food recommender systems: important contributions, challenges and future research directions. arXiv preprint arXiv:1711.02760.
  4. Ge, M., Ricci, F. and Massimo, D., 2015, September. Health-aware food recommender system. In Proceedings of the 9th ACM Conference on Recommender Systems (pp. 333-334).

A Storage and Annotation Engine for Researchers

Keywords: Information Retrieval Research Web Programming

You will use the CORE API (https://core.ac.uk/services/api/) in order to build a search engine client that lets the user annotate articles and create a personal database. The search engine client will let the user add a paper to their personal collection, which will download them into an offline document collection where the paper will be indexed, along with their annotation, for further use.

A Personalised Plot Generator

Keywords: Machine Learning Computational Creativity Web Programming

In this project you will build a system that generates a movie plot based on specific personal features of the user. The user will then receive a set of candidate plots and be able to mark them up or down, based on a specific set of criteria (whether they like it, whether it makes sense, whether it has too many grammatical mistakes). The system will then use that feedback to generate new movie plots. The system will then be enhanced to allow for cross-user recommendation, by providing a user with plots from other users in order to build a collaborative filtering profile, and use that collective information to write better plots.

An Active-Learning Based Labeling Application

Keywords: Machine Learning Active Learning Web Programming

[Taken by a student. If you are interested get in touch and we can work on designing a variation of it]

Creating a good dataset is one of the major pain points when investigating new venues in machine learning. One of the reasons for this is that labelling data is difficult and time-consuming, and software designed to ease that process is scarce and expensive. This project aims at producing an open source alternative to those solutions, where the designer of the experiment can configure a hosted personalised labelling software in a couple of hours. The application would then allow authorised users to log in a Web interface, and be presented with a sequence of random examples that they can label, which is where the research part of the project is. One of the goals of the project is to investigate and develop a set of ordering heuristics that let the system know which instance to present to the user for labelling. This can depend on intrinsic features of the data (text, image, audio) or features derived from more complex models (e.g. distance from separating hyperplane).

This is not the only way to do things well, but it is a way that does things well consistently, and therefore I am more comfortable in sharing it.
